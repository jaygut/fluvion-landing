Flying Rivers

Two Andean capitals drink rain that begins far to the east. Moisture the Amazon rainforest breathes into the sky rides the wind up the mountains and falls on the high paramo grasslands that feed Quito and Bogota. The same engine that priced an Amazon-to-soy corridor now follows that water back to its source and measures how much of each city's rain the forest makes.

Quito · the Amazon-facing tower

More of Quito's rain is made in the Amazon than anywhere else.

Of the rain that falls on the high grasslands feeding Quito, the Amazon is the single largest source, in a typical year. Those grasslands drain east toward the Amazon, and the prevailing wind carries Amazon moisture straight up the eastern slope. On the map, the teal ribbon's width is that measured share, drawn from the western Amazon into the water tower.

The resolution

A region, not a pixel.

The source map works in degree squares, about km across near the equator. A paramo intake is far smaller than one square, so this is a useful regional attribution, never a per-pixel or single-drought number. We show exactly what is measured and what is not: the attribution and the seasonal check are done; the water-supply price stays locked until observed hydrology is in. Any future valuation would anchor to the real protected paramo, never a grid square.

Not a forecast; not an attribution of any single drought. RECON is a 2008-2017 climatology with no year-to-year signal.
