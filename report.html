The Amazon's rain is worth $20 billion a year. We traced it to a single forest and priced the risk.

Amazon forests breathe water into the sky, and the wind carries it a thousand miles to rain on the soy fields that feed global supply chains. UK pension funds and banks are exposed to that link. Science has valued that rain, but nobody has traced it back to a specific forest and priced the risk it carries. Fluvion does: it follows the flying rivers from forest to harvest, validates the chain against 22 years of harvest records, and turns it into two numbers a lender can use. What the risk is worth. What the forest is worth for holding the rain in place. And because a forest is not a generic pipe, it grades the source as well as pricing the dependency: how intact the forest is decides not how much rain it makes, but how reliably it makes it.

⚙How the engine works

It helps to know what is under the hood, because the credibility rests on it. The engine is not a black box, and it is not new physics. It is a chain of peer-reviewed science, wired together and run as one calculation so the uncertainty carries through from end to end, then checked against what actually happened.

It runs in one direction, then reverses. Forward, it follows the water: how much moisture a forest sends downwind, how much of that rain is lost if the forest degrades, how much yield the missing rain costs, and how much money that is to a UK book. Backward, it flips the same chain to ask what the forest is worth, per hectare, for preventing that loss. Each link is an established relationship from the published literature, not something we invented.

The first link is the heavy one, and it is why this is not a laptop model. Tracing the moisture a forest releases to the rain it becomes a thousand miles downwind is the job of atmospheric moisture-tracking models, WAM2layers and UTrack, that follow water vapour through the sky as the winds carry it. They run on ERA5, the European reanalysis that reconstructs the global atmosphere hour by hour across decades and spans several petabytes. We remote-subset the reanalysis to the corridor rather than move the whole archive, and the tracking itself is high-performance-computing work, run on cluster nodes with hundreds of gigabytes of memory and terabytes of local scratch, not on a desk machine. From runs like these come the moisture-flow products the engine stands on, three moisture-tracking products that cross-check one another (they share the same ERA5 winds, so this is internal consistency, not full independence). Everything downstream is lighter: a crop-rainfall response measured by others from decades of official harvest and rainfall records, a standard financial translation, and a fifty-thousand-run Monte Carlo that carries the uncertainty through to the answer. This is the shape of the moat: the system is expensive to build and cheap to serve. Producing a validated corridor is supercomputer-scale work; serving it is the calculator above, the same open model run in your browser in milliseconds.

Three choices are what make it trustworthy rather than merely clever. We borrow the science instead of re-deriving it, so every physical step is someone else's peer-reviewed result, cited in the audit trail. We run the whole chain thousands of times across the uncertainty in every input, so the answer is always a calibrated range, never a single hopeful number. And we test it against twenty-two years of real harvests before asking anyone to trust it. The part that is genuinely ours is the integration and the validation, not the physics. None of it is secret, and that is deliberate: the recipe is public so the number can be audited, while the barrier to copying it is the work itself, the petabyte-scale compute, the multi-model ensemble, and the multi-year backtest that stand behind every validated corridor.

2The gap nobody fills

Every water and nature risk tool on the market scores the danger where the asset sits. The field. The mill. The river running past it. None of them trace the moisture back to the forest that made the rain in the first place. Putting a price on nature risk is not new, and we do not pretend it is. Following the rain upwind to its source, and pricing that link, is. It is the one column the whole industry leaves blank. There is a second blank beside it: even the tools that name a dependency on nature do not grade the condition of what is depended on, whether the source is intact and steady or thinning and volatile. Fluvion fills both, and we find no other tool that fills either.

3The flying river

5We checked it against history

A model is only worth trusting if it matches what already happened. So we tested ours against the real harvests, state by state, year by year. In the La Niña drought of 2021 and 2022, the south of Brazil collapsed and the centre held. The engine reproduces that split. It also passes on 2024, a year we held out a priori, before any scoring.

We are honest about what this proves. The engine gets the pattern right, which states fall and which hold, not the exact size of each drop. A simple yearly rule cannot fully capture a 58 percent collapse in Rio Grande do Sul from a 13 percent shortfall in rain. The strength of the result rests on about 22 separate years of evidence, not on any single year: the average year-on-year correlation is about 0.3, with roughly a one in 250 chance of being luck. Some calmer years show no signal at all. The engine is a drought detector, not a crystal ball, and we say so.

◆Not all hectares are equal: the grade of the source

Everything so far has priced the dependency: what the downstream harvest is worth, and what the forest is worth for holding the rain in place. But a forest is not a pipe with a single number. It has a condition, and the condition decides not how much rain it makes, but how reliably it makes it. This is the second half of the thesis: grade the source, price the dependency.

The science here is narrow, and we keep it narrow on purpose, because the easy version is wrong. A hostile reviewer would dismantle "more biodiversity, more rain" in one question, and they would be right to. What the evidence actually says is about stability, not average. In a process-based simulation, a forest richer in hydraulic traits loses far less biomass under a severe drought, and the authors are explicit that mean transpiration does not rise with diversity: the gain is a fall in the standard deviation of the water flux (Langan et al. 2025, Nature Communications 16:8246). Drier, degraded forests are measurably less diverse and slower to respond, with less of the redundancy that buffers function after a shock (Aguirre-Gutierrez et al. 2022, Nature Ecology and Evolution 6:878), and degraded patches lose evaporation stability under exactly the hot, dry extremes that matter while intact high-biomass patches hold (Longo et al. 2025, ERL 20(5)). So an intact, diverse forest is a steadier supplier of the rain its downwind assets depend on. A degraded one delivers the same average in a calm year and falls short when it is hit.

The one line a sharp reviewer will test, so we state it first: biodiversity and intactness stabilize the water service, lowering its variance and its drought-tail. They do not raise its mean. We never say more biodiversity makes more rain, and the grade never lifts the $350.

That null on the average is what lets the grade map cleanly onto a risk number instead of fudging it. A Water-VaR is a statement about a loss distribution, a middle and a spread. The forest's share of the rain, w_soybelt, sets the middle, and that is where the $350 lives, unchanged. Source condition sets the spread and, because resilience is drifting, the direction of travel:

Intact, diverse, redundant source

Holds its evaporation through drought. A smaller drought-year shortfall, a thinner downside. Resilience flat or recovering.

A steadier, thinner-tailed supply. The same average service, a narrower loss distribution. A lower-Water-VaR source to depend on.

Degraded, thinning, resilience-losing source

Loses evaporation when it is hit. A larger drought-year shortfall, a fatter downside. Resilience trending down, and self-amplifying.

The same average today, a worse downside and a worse trajectory. A higher-Water-VaR, declining-quality water asset.

What this is, and what it is not. This is not a multiplier we bolt onto the price. There is no source-grade term in the $350, in the VaR, or in w_soybelt today, and the central $350 will never carry a grade multiplier. Source condition enters in two honest places only: a descriptive monitor that shows the source's observed condition beside the price (the next section), and a research coupling, a link from source condition to downwind-rainfall variance, that no published study has yet estimated. We state it as mechanism-supported and to be validated, never as demonstrated.

↗Where this goes: from a price to a signal

The last section said source condition is a variance and trend term, not a price multiplier. This section is how we observe it. The $350 is a static value, audited against twenty-two years of history; making it live means watching the grade of the source behind it and re-stating the risk as that grade moves. The proposal grows along two axes. This is the first: depth, making the price live. The next section is the second: breadth, more corridors. We move down the depth axis one validated step at a time, and we are precise about what is built, what is buildable now, and what still has to be earned.

The grade is a moving quantity, and it is moving the wrong way. Resilience has fallen across most of the Amazon since the early 2000s, fastest in the drier, human-proximate west where this corridor's source sits (Boulton et al. 2022), and the 2023 to 2024 drought set records in those same signatures (Buras et al. 2026). The clearest bridge from source condition to downwind rain runs through our own physics: a 2026 Nature study applies UTrack, the same moisture-tracking model behind Fluvion's S1, and finds that deforestation dries the forest's own rainfall, with roughly two-thirds of the Amazon at risk of shifting toward a degraded state below 2 degrees of warming once clearing reaches about 22 to 28 percent, against the roughly 17 to 18 percent already lost (Wunderling et al. 2026). That is why a monitor, not a one-time report, is the right instrument: the thing we grade is drifting.

How we keep ourselves honest. Before we showed this signal we asked whether the 2023 and 2024 evaporation collapse was an artifact of one model, and we fixed the pass and fail line in advance. With two products the cross-check was weak (0.49). We added the reanalysis leg we had named, the mean correlation crossed to 0.56, and our first rule called that robust. Then an adversarial review pushed back, and it was right: that mean is carried by the two water-balance-family models (TerraClimate and ERA5), which share forcing, and it is inflated by the very 2023 and 2024 years the verdict is about. Strip those two years out and the correlation falls to 0.48; on the pre-2023 record it is 0.35; the one genuinely independent leg, the satellite, never clears the line. So we did not keep the robust label, and we did not move the 0.50 line either. We added the stricter rules the reviewer asked for, report the correlation under all of them, and downgraded to partly corroborated: the sign agreement in 2023 and 2024 is the strong, real result, and the year-to-year correlation is weak. We also show the weakest number we could have buried: month to month the streams barely track (0.19). What a careful reviewer asks first.
Is the collapse just a TerraClimate artifact? No. Two further products, a satellite (MODIS MYD16A2GF) and a reanalysis (ERA5), show the same source-box dry-season fall in both 2023 and 2024. Three paradigms agree in direction; we report the magnitude and correlation gaps openly, and we treat only the satellite as a genuinely independent observation.
Is it robust, or partly corroborated? Partly corroborated. Our rule needs the correlation to clear 0.50 under stricter rules, not just the mean. It clears only as a mean (0.56), carried by the two co-forced models (TerraClimate and ERA5, 0.76); under the median (0.49), the weakest pair (0.43), and a holdout that excludes the 2023 and 2024 events (0.48), it does not. The strong, real result is the sign agreement in both years. We did not move the threshold; we report the correlation under every rule and label it partly corroborated, not robust. It is a cross-paradigm term, not a validation of the price and not a forecast.
Are the three paradigms truly independent? Only the satellite is. It reads the surface from orbit. The water-balance model (TerraClimate) and the reanalysis (ERA5) are both precipitation-and-radiation-forced over the same box, so their high agreement (0.76) is shared-forcing coherence, not two independent instruments converging. We lean on the satellite for genuine independence, and it sits below the line at 0.43 to 0.49, which is exactly why the verdict is partly corroborated, not robust.
Why does the box barely move in the 2015 to 2016 drought? It is a western-Amazon box, and that El Niño stressed the eastern and southern Amazon more. The 2023 to 2024 basin-wide drought reached it. This is descriptive scope, the place the monitor watches, not a claim that it knows which droughts matter.
Is the satellite leg only a few pixels? It is a box-representative sample of three points (the open subset service is rate limited), at the same locations as the greenness audit, not a full-box raster. We use it as a tie-breaker on direction, not as a magnitude anchor.

8What we do not claim

The honesty is the point. We cannot yet prove how much rain a given amount of deforestation removes, because real droughts come from many causes at once, so we report the forest value as a range, not a single figure. The risk number is the uncertainty in the average yearly loss, not a worst case tail. We never claim a calibrated 95 percent confidence off a handful of events, only an honest floor. And the $350 is a floor itself: water, one crop, one place the rain lands. The real number is larger. We are equally careful on the source grade: we do not claim that biodiversity makes more rain. The defensible claim is narrower, that an intact, diverse forest holds its output more steadily under drought, a lower variance and a thinner tail, not a higher average, and we never let source condition lift the $350. It is a variance and trend term, and a coupling still to be validated. On the forward roadmap we hold the same line: the condition monitor describes what the forest is doing, it does not forecast the harvest, and nothing earns the words forecast or early warning until a published hindcast beats the simple baselines at a stated lead time.

Each valuation is computed once, from open peer-reviewed science and named datasets, then published in two forms from that single source. For people, it becomes the investor report you are reading, a one-page corridor tear sheet for the investment committee, and a live calculator to flex scenarios. For software, the identical figures become a structured, signed risk object that other systems and AI agents can query over an API or an MCP endpoint, and verify directly. Every figure travels with its provenance and its confidence, so a person can read it and an automated system can act on it or audit it, without anyone re-deriving the science. Nothing in the two views can drift apart, because both render the same signed result.
