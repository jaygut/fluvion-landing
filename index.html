Flying rivers

Flying Rivers

A forest in the Amazon breathes water into the sky. Weeks later it falls as rain on farms two thousand kilometres south. We follow that water back to its source, and put a price on the risk.

The blind spot

Half of Britain's nature risk starts abroad, and some of it blows in.

The Green Finance Institute puts up to 12 percent of UK GDP at risk from nature loss, and roughly half of it starts overseas. Part of it rides the wind, as moisture one forest releases and another region drinks in. We could find no risk tool that follows that water back to its source, and none that grades the source it depends on: whether the forest making the rain is intact and steady, or thinning and volatile.

Where the water starts

Three forests in Amazonas. 324,542 hectares.

Three standing forests in the western Amazon, 324,542 hectares between them. Every year they lift billions of tonnes of water into the air. The soy of southern South America, downwind, treats that as free rainfall: it has never paid for it, never insured it, and never asked whether the forest behind it is holding up.

The flying river

About a fifth of the La Plata basin's rain comes from the Amazon.

We took it from the moisture-tracking record, the kind of petabyte-scale atmospheric reanalysis and moisture-transport modelling that runs on supercomputers, not spreadsheets, and reconciled it to the physics for this corridor. The Amazon hands the La Plata basin 21.5 percent of its rainfall, right inside the range the published science expects. This is a supply line into one of the world's great breadbaskets.

The proof

In 2022 the rivers ran thin, and the map turned red where it should.

The La Niña drought of 2021 and 2022 cut Rio Grande do Sul's soy by more than half while the cerrado held steady. Our engine reproduces that split, which states fall and which hold. It also clears the bar on 2024, a year we held out a priori, before any scoring. This is a backtest, not a forecast.

The prize

As a rain machine, this forest is worth $350 a hectare.

That is the value of the harvests it quietly protects downstream, counted over thirty years. It already beats the $295 a hectare the land itself sells for, the regional average from Brazil's land registry. And it counts only water, only soy, only one place the rain lands. The full number is larger.

The grade

A forest losing resilience makes the same rain, less reliably.

The evidence points one way: a forest's condition does not change how much rain it makes on average, it changes how steadily it makes it. Intact, diverse forest is documented to hold its transpiration through drought, while degraded, resilience-losing forest wavers under the same heat. We read that condition from observed evaporation volatility and published resilience indicators, and we treat it as what it is: a descriptive, lagging correlate we are still validating, not a proven driver the price already carries. So it moves only the spread and the drought tail, never the $350, and it sits beside the price, never inside it.

Where this goes

A price today. A live signal next.

We already watch that grade. From open satellite records we track the source forest's condition and read it as it changes, and the trend is not flat: the western Amazon has lost resilience for two decades, and the 2023 and 2024 droughts drove its evaporation sharply below normal. That sits beside the price, never inside it, until the link to the harvest is earned. Further out, a research path tests whether we can warn a season ahead, where the El Niño cycle is documented to lend the growing season some seasonal skill, and we will publish that skill before we ever call it a forecast.

The ask

One corridor, validated. Two ways to back it.

One open core and one commercial layer, built by one team as a single venture. The open engine is the auditable standard underneath; the commercial layer prices it and serves it, to an investment desk or an AI agent, by API. The same engine reads two ways: it prices the dependency for the desks that carry the downside, and it grades the source for the capital that protects it. The science is built, validated, and checked line by line for one corridor. Scaling it to the world's flying rivers is a compute problem: dozens of corridors, each its own supercomputing run and validation campaign, which is what backing this builds. The next move is yours.
